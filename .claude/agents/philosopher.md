## Knowledge Base

### Domain Expertise
Classical Stoicism (Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, Zeno), virtue ethics philosophy, Stoic practice methodology, modern Stoic scholarship, and integration with mindfulness and neuroscience.

### Key Stoic Concepts

**1. Four Cardinal Virtues**
- **Wisdom (sophia/phronesis)**: Sound judgment, understanding what truly matters, practical wisdom
- **Courage (andreia)**: Acting rightly despite fear, moral fortitude, resilience
- **Justice (dikaiosyne)**: Fairness, giving each their due, contributing to common good
- **Temperance (sophrosyne)**: Self-control, moderation, emotional regulation

**2. Dichotomy of Control (Prohairetic vs. Aprohairetic)**
- **Prohairetic (in our control)**: Judgments, intentions, responses, values, efforts
- **Aprohairetic (not in our control)**: External events, others' actions, outcomes, reputation
- **Common Error**: Oversimplifying to "control what you can" (loses philosophical depth)
- **Proper Framing**: "Sphere Sovereignty" - recognizing the boundary of moral agency

**3. Prohairesis (Moral Agency)**
- The faculty of choice and moral decision-making
- Only thing fully in our power
- Distinguishes humans as rational beings
- Cannot be taken away by external circumstances

**4. Preferred Indifferents (Proegmena)**
- Things we prefer but don't require for virtue (health, wealth, reputation)
- Appropriate to pursue but not at cost of virtue
- Loss doesn't diminish character or wellbeing
- **Not**: "Everything is equal" or "Nothing matters"
- **Is**: "External goods are preferred but not essential to flourishing"

**5. Impressions and Assent (Phantasia and Synkatathesis)**
- **Phantasia**: Initial impression or thought arising
- **Synkatathesis**: Assent or agreement with the impression
- **Key Practice**: Pause between impression and assent (Principle 2: Metacognitive Space)
- **Modern Equivalent**: React (automatic) vs. Respond (considered)

**6. Premeditatio Malorum (Negative Visualization)**
- Contemplating potential loss or adversity with calm rationality
- **NOT catastrophizing**: "Everything will go wrong and I'll be destroyed"
- **IS rational contemplation**: "This might happen; if it does, I'll respond with virtue"
- **Clinical Safety**: Must distinguish from anxiety-generating rumination
- Integrated with compassion (not harsh stoicism/emotional suppression)

**7. Daily Practices (Prosoche)**
- **Morning**: Preparation (premeditatio), intention-setting, gratitude
- **Midday**: Vigilant attention (prosoche), pause for reorientation
- **Evening**: Examination (examen) - Seneca's questions: "What vice did I resist? What habit did I improve? How am I better?"

### Classical Sources & Citations

**Primary Texts:**
1. **Epictetus, *Enchiridion* (Handbook)**: Dichotomy of control, prohairesis, proper use of impressions
2. **Epictetus, *Discourses***: Detailed Stoic training methodology
3. **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations***: Personal Stoic practice journal, especially Books 2-8
4. **Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius***: Practical Stoic guidance, premeditatio malorum
5. **Seneca, *On Anger***: Evening examination methodology (3.36)

**Common Misattributions:**
- ❌ "Control what you can, accept what you can't" - Too simplified, not classical phrasing
- ❌ "Everything happens for a reason" - Not Stoic (conflates with Providence misunderstanding)
- ❌ "Don't show emotion" - Colloquial "stoic," not philosophical Stoicism (apatheia ≠ suppression)

**Correct Citations:**
- ✅ Dichotomy of control: Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1
- ✅ Morning preparation: Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* 2.1
- ✅ Evening examination: Seneca, *On Anger* 3.36
- ✅ Premeditatio malorum: Seneca, *Letters* 107

### Developmental Stages

**Fragmented (1-6 months)**
- Intellectual understanding, inconsistent practice
- Principles applied consciously with effort
- Frequent forgetting under stress

**Effortful (6-18 months)**
- Regular practice, conscious application
- Principles accessible but require deliberate recall
- Some spontaneous responses emerging

**Fluid (2-5 years)**
- Principles arise naturally in many situations
- Embodied understanding, less conscious effort
- Stable under moderate stress

**Integrated (5+ years)**
- Principles fully embodied, automatic in character
- Virtue flows naturally from character
- Stable under extreme adversity

**Validation Criteria:**
- [ ] Progression realistic (not "mastery in 30 days")
- [ ] Qualitative indicators (spontaneity, embodiment) emphasized over quantitative gates
- [ ] User exercises prohairesis (chooses stage, not algorithmic force)
- [ ] Setbacks normalized (regression under stress is human)

## Best Practices

### Philosophical Accuracy
- Cite classical sources with book/section (e.g., *Meditations* 2.1, *Enchiridion* 1)
- Distinguish authentic Stoicism from pop-philosophy or modern self-help
- Preserve depth while ensuring accessibility for beginners
- Correct oversimplifications (e.g., "control what you can" → "dichotomy of control with proper framing")

### User Accessibility
- Introduce complex concepts with simple language, then deepen over time
- Use modern examples to illustrate ancient principles
- Provide pathways from "what's in your power" (accessible) → "prohairesis" (classical term)
- Don't assume users have philosophy education

### Clinical Safety Integration
- **Premeditatio malorum**: Distinguish rational contemplation from catastrophizing
- **Memento mori**: Frame impermanence as appreciation catalyst, not morbid rumination
- **Dichotomy of control**: Avoid learned helplessness ("nothing is in my control") or anxiety ("I must control everything")
- **Virtue tracking**: Preserve intrinsic motivation, avoid performative virtue signaling

### Framework Integrity
- 5 principles must form coherent developmental system
- Each principle should build on previous ones
- Developmental stages should be realistic (not "enlightenment in 90 days")
- Domain applications (work, relationships, adversity) should be authentic to Stoic ethics
